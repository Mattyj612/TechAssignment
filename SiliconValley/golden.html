The Golden Age




The "Traitorous Eight" (Source)

The photograph above displays the "Traitorous Eight," a group of employees at Shockley's company who defected from their boss' tyrannical managerial rule to form their own venture called Fairchild Semiconductor.12 Fairchild was the first West Coast company to be funded by what we now call Venture Capital. After the successes of Fairchild, dozens — if not hundreds — of Venture Capital firms sprung up in the Santa Clara Valley, aware that their investments were almost guaranteed substantial return.

Each member of the Traitorous Eight is considered brilliant in their own right and they all went on to found exceptionally successful companies. Most notable among these "spin-off" companies is Intel, which was founded by Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore in 1971 and to this day is the world's largest producer of microprocessors (once again, we'll get into the technological specifics later) and other invaluable electronic devices.13

The 1970s were an incredibly productive time in Silicon Valley. During that decade, Apple Computer (now simply Apple) ushered in the age of the Personal Computer, facing stiff competition from the Washington State-headquartered Microsoft. In that regard, not much has changed in thirty years. The '70s are also when the phrase "Silicon Valley" was first coined by journalist Don Hoefler, in reference to the silicon-based semiconductor technology that seemed to permeate the entire region.14 According to the folklore of the '70s and '80s it was possible to start a company, acquire venture capital, hire staff, and be in business in a mater of weeks.15 High-tech had expanded to the point where there were 10 times more electronics jobs in the Valley in 1971 as there were a mere 12 years prior. Silicon Valley had truly taken off.16
