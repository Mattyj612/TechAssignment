Early History




Our story begins in 1849, when California was the site of a large gold rush. During this profitable time, wealth-seekers both domestic and foreign frolicked to San Francisco in hopes of quite literally striking it rich.3 As a result of this dramatic population influx — almost 300,000 new residents arrived during the decade after the gold rush — California was given US statehood in 1850.4 In those early days, California was (and continues to be) seen as a fresh start, a place to make a new living in an incomparable climate, far away from the polluted industry of New York or the cotton-drive economy of the South. In some sense, with the arrival of the first prospector, the spirit of Silicon Valley had already been born.

Stanford University (Source)


After Gold, the next pivotal precursor to the Valley was the founding of Stanford University in 1885.5 Established on Leland Stanford's farmland adjacent to Palo Alto, the University's purpose was to foster self-sufficient local industry in the burgeoning and ever-expanding "Golden" state. It also sought to be a west-coast competitor to the likes of Harvard, Yale, and MIT, and was designed to become the central academic institution in the Bay Area.

Taken together, the Gold Rush and the existence of a well-funded, meticulously designed University helped establish California as a land of unique opportunity both financially and intellectually. The merger of these two ideals allowed for profitable science and income-driven innovation.
