Pre-Transistor




Frederick Terman, the Father of Silicon Valley (Source)

The man in the photograph to the left is Frederick Terman, who is generally considered the "Father of Silicon Valley."6 After receiving a PhD from MIT in 1924, he returned to Stanford, where he had done his undergraduate degree, and became a leading expert on radio engineering.7 More importantly, he was passionate about innovation and actively sought out bright young minds to take under his wing. He quickly found two of the brightest, who would go on to found Silicon Valley's first important company: Hewlett-Packard (HP).

HP was founded in 1939 by William Hewlett and Dave Packard, who worked in a worn-down garage developing an electronic device which now produces the signals used in radio and television transmission. As the company grew, corporate culture evolved into what has been dubbed "The HP Way," a managerial philosophy which emphasizes individual brainpower and ignores hierarchy.8 This set the groundwork for the oft-parodied tie-less, sandle-clad CEO who has come to typify Silicon Valley management.

The second big company to emerge under Terman's early watch was Varian Associates, founded by brothers Russell and Sigurd Varian in 1948. They developed a device straight out of science fiction called the klystron, which like Hewlett and Packard's creation found immediate application in telecommunications as well as theoretical physics.
