The Transistor




It's been said the alchemy of Silicon Valley is turning sand into gold.21 Transistors are nail-sized pieces made out of silicon, the primary component of sand, and they play a role in every single modern electronic device. They are based on the idea of a semiconductor, which is a type of material that can selectively conduct electrons and therefore behave like a switch. In the '50s semiconductor manufacturers were virtually celebrities.22

It's not easy to overstate the importance of transistors. They are as ubiquitous as technology gets. Looking around the room in which I write this paragraph, I see a microwave, television, satellite, cell phone, computer, iPod, and innumerable other devices which could never have become this widespread without transistors. They are the namesake of Silicon Valley, and have remained the crowning achievement of 20th century high-tech innovation. In terms of the future, there has yet to be an improvement made on the transistor. Many have tried and failed, but nanotechnology research has yet to produce a smaller device which operates so flawlessly and which is so elegantly designed.
