Geographical Overview




To the right you can see a map of Silicon Valley. Feel free to play around before reading on! Google Earth View, which can be activated by clicking the bottom-left corner of the map, is particularly informative since it emphasizes the distinction between natural and developed lands.

Silicon Valley itself is not technically a city. The name refers to a collection of cities in the San Francisco Bay Area tucked in the lowly Santa Clara Valley between the imposing Santa Cruz Mountains to the West and the Diablo Range to the East. Paolo Alto and San Jose make up its largest individual cities and are generally considered to bound it to the North and South, respectively.

Prior to the mid-20th century, almost all of this territory was used for agriculture. The area was dubbed "The Valley of Heart's Delight" and was known for its high-quality cherries, apricots, and almonds.1 Today, some of this farmland still exists, especially toward the mountainous periphery, but commercial and industrial developments are a much more common sight in the Valley.

The total population is between 3.5-4 million, depending on which specific towns are considered a part Silicon Valley.2 For comparison, this is approximately the size of Los Angeles. The climate is typical Californian coastal fare, with palm treed abound and ne'er a snowy day in sight.

When considering these geographic factors in tandem, it makes perfect sense that Silicon Valley would one day become today's start-up hotbed. A near perfect climate and uncharted farmlands enticed brave-souled individuals to make the trek, and once they arrived autonomy and independence were guaranteed by the surrounding mountains and beaches.
