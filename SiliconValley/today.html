Today




Silicon Valley, Circa 1900 (Source)

It's difficult to imagine that in less than a century Silicon Valley transformed from the scenic view on the left to the dense, corporate-heavy start-up haven it is today. The establishment of Stanford University, the championing of local industry by Frederick Terman, and the foundation of Shockley Semiconductor were among the principal formative events that allowed what was once a cherry farmer's dream to become a playground for Apple. Silicon Valley currently receives 40% of all startup capital in the United States; second on that list is New England at a meager 12%17 On Fortune 400's list of richest people in America, Silicon Valley-affiliated entrepreneurs make up 7 out of the top 20, beating out oil barons, investment bankers, and even Warren Buffet.18 Perhaps a revision on the biblical adage is in order: the geek shall inherit the earth.

There have, however, been some challenges associated with the rise of Silicon Valley. Chief among them is that residents of San Jose and surrounding regions who do not work at high-tech companies often can't afford many basic necessities. Prices are dramatically inflated in Silicon Valley, driven up by the mass quantity of concentrated wealth.19 Housing, for example, is disproportionately expensive relative to the rest of California. In response to this perceived injustice, many San Francisco Bay Area activists have staged protests on Google's private shuttle bus service, which takes employees from their San Francisco homes to the core of Silicon Valley.20 Not much has come of these demonstration as far as policy shifts, though they have emphasized that the non-entrepreneurial Silicon Valley population plays an equally important role in the community.
